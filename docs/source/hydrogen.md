# Hydrogen Modeling in ReEDS

This document provides detailed documentation for the representation of hydrogen (H<sub>2</sub>) production, transportation, storage, consumption, emissions, and policy incentives in the ReEDS capacity expansion model. For a summary-level treatment, see the [Hydrogen section](model_documentation.md#hydrogen) of the main model documentation.

## Overview

ReEDS includes a comprehensive representation of the hydrogen economy's intersection with the electric power system. The model captures:

- **Production** of hydrogen via electrolysis, steam methane reforming (SMR), and SMR with carbon capture and storage (CCS).
- **Consumption** of hydrogen as a fuel in hydrogen combustion turbines (H<sub>2</sub>-CTs) and hydrogen combined cycles (H<sub>2</sub>-CCs), as well as exogenous demand from non-power sectors (e.g., transportation, industry).
- **Transport** of hydrogen between model regions via pipelines, with explicit pipeline investment, compressor costs, and transmission losses.
- **Storage** of hydrogen in geological formations (salt caverns, hard rock) and engineered underground pipe systems.
- **Emissions** from hydrogen leakage across the supply chain, upstream methane emissions from SMR, and the integration of hydrogen's global warming potential into CO<sub>2</sub>-equivalent emission caps.
- **Policy** incentives including the Clean Hydrogen Production Tax Credit (Section 45V of the U.S. tax code).

The scope and spatial resolution of the hydrogen representation is controlled by the `GSw_H2` switch:

| `GSw_H2` | Mode | Description |
|:---------:|:-----|:------------|
| `0` | Drop-in fuel | No endogenous production. H<sub>2</sub> is available at a fixed exogenous price. |
| `1` | National balancing | Endogenous production via electrolysis and/or SMR. Supply and demand are balanced annually at the national level. A flat adder represents transport and storage costs. |
| `2` | Regional balancing (default) | Endogenous production with hourly, zonal supply-demand balance. Explicit pipeline transport network and geological storage investments. |

## Hydrogen Combustion Technologies

Hydrogen can be consumed in the power sector by two generator types: hydrogen combustion turbines (H<sub>2</sub>-CTs) and hydrogen combined cycles (H<sub>2</sub>-CCs). These are comparable to commercial natural gas plants but are designed to combust hydrogen fuel (Mitsubishi (2020); Ruth et al. (2020)).

**Cost and performance assumptions.** H<sub>2</sub>-CTs and H<sub>2</sub>-CCs are assumed to have the same heat rate and operation and maintenance (O&M) costs as their natural gas counterparts (see the [Fossil and Nuclear Technologies](model_documentation.md#fossil-and-nuclear-technologies) section of the main documentation) but with a 10% higher overnight capital cost to allow the turbine to function as a synchronous generator Ruth et al. (2020). Cost and performance trajectories are specified in `inputs/plant_characteristics/h2-combustion_ATB_2024.csv` (selected via `plantchar_h2combustion`), which provides `capcost`, `fom`, `vom`, and `heatrate` for each technology and year. This file is processed by `input_processing/plantcostprep.py` and merged with other generator types for GAMS.

**Heat rate adjustment for low capacity factors.** H<sub>2</sub>-CCs are expected to operate at lower capacity factors than baseload gas combined cycles. Using monthly plant-level data from EIA Forms 860 and 923 (EIA (2024); EIA (2024)), an empirical relationship between capacity factor and heat rate was developed for the existing NG-CC fleet. The analysis shows that shifting from a mean fleet capacity factor of 51% to the 6% minimum-CF constraint in ReEDS results in an 11.5% higher heat rate. This penalty is applied as a heat rate modifier for H<sub>2</sub>-CCs.

**Retrofit pathways.** Existing natural gas plants can be upgraded to hydrogen combustion:

- **Gas-CT → H<sub>2</sub>-CT**: Pays 33% of the difference in capital cost between the two technologies. This fraction is derived from the combustion turbine "mechanical—major equipment" cost share (\$54M of \$166M total, per EIA capital cost estimates for F-class turbines).
- **Gas-CC → H<sub>2</sub>-CC**: Pays 28% of the cost difference, derived similarly for an H-class 2×2×1 combined cycle (\$294M of \$1,038M total).

> **Hydrogen combustion switches**
>
> - `GSw_H2Combustion` (default `1`): Enable (`1`) or disable (`0`) H<sub>2</sub>-CT and H<sub>2</sub>-CC technologies.
> - `GSw_H2CombinedCycle` (default `1`): Enable (`1`) or disable (`0`) H<sub>2</sub>-CC specifically.
> - `GSw_H2Combustionupgrade` (default `1`): Allow (`1`) or prevent (`0`) upgrades of existing gas-CT and gas-CC to H<sub>2</sub> combustion.
> - `plantchar_h2combustion` (default `h2-combustion_ATB_2024`): Selects the cost and performance input file.


## Hydrogen Production

### Drop-In Renewable Fuel (`GSw_H2=0`)

The simplest representation models hydrogen as a drop-in renewable fuel available in unlimited quantity at a fixed price. The default fuel cost is \$20/MMBtu, consistent with estimates of hydrogen produced via electrolysis powered by dedicated wind or solar Mahone et al. (2020), and within the range of current ethanol (\$12/MMBtu) and biodiesel (\$30/MMBtu) prices DOE (2020). This all-in cost includes production, delivery, and storage. Users can select alternative price trajectories via the `h2combustionfuelscen` switch (options: `10`, `reference` [\$20/MMBtu], `30`), which selects the corresponding file from `inputs/fuelprices/h2-combustion_{scenario}.csv`. Each file contains a simple `year,cost` time series.

Under this approach, the use of curtailed renewable energy for hydrogen production is not explicitly captured. To model that dynamic, endogenous production must be enabled (`GSw_H2=1` or `GSw_H2=2`).

### Electrolysis

Electrolyzers consume electricity to produce hydrogen. Cost and performance inputs are specified in `inputs/consume/consume_char_{ref,low}.csv` (selected via `GSw_H2_Inputs`), which contains electrolyzer, SMR, and SMR-CCS data with parameters `cost_cap`, `fom`, `vom`, `ele_efficiency`, `gas_efficiency`, and `stortran_adder` by year. This file is processed by `input_processing/plantcostprep.py`, which adjusts the capital cost to include the present-value cost of electrolyzer stack replacement before writing the GAMS-ready `consume_char.csv` to `inputs_case/`. ReEDS represents electrolysis with the following characteristics:

- **Capital cost**: Declines from \$1,750/kW (2020) to \$550/kW (2035 onward) in \$2022.
- **Efficiency**: Improves from 56.1 kWh/kg H<sub>2</sub> (2020) to 51.5 kWh/kg (2050).
- **Stack replacement**: A one-time cost of 60% of installed capital cost (scalar `h2_elec_stack_replace_perc`) is incurred after 10 years (scalar `h2_elec_stack_replace_year`) to replace the PEM electrolyzer stack. `plantcostprep.py` adds this as a discounted present-value adder to the `cost_cap` trajectory.
- **Lifespan**: 20 years.

The conversion between electricity consumption and hydrogen output is governed by the `prod_conversion_rate` parameter (GAMS: `prod_conversion_rate(i,v,r,t)`), which is the inverse of the electrolyzer's electricity use (in metric tons H<sub>2</sub> per MWh). Electrolyzer electricity consumption is added directly to the system load balance constraint (`eq_loadcon`): the model must dispatch sufficient generation (or import power via transmission) to serve both conventional load and electrolyzer demand in every region and time period.

### Steam Methane Reforming (SMR)

Steam methane reforming converts natural gas to hydrogen. ReEDS can represent SMR with or without carbon capture:

- **SMR** (without CCS): Capital cost of \$649/kW (2020), natural gas consumption of 0.192 MMBtu/kg H<sub>2</sub>, and process CO<sub>2</sub> emissions of 9.83 metric tons CO<sub>2</sub> per metric ton H<sub>2</sub> produced. A 10% forced outage rate is assumed.
- **SMR with CCS**: Capital cost of \$1,408/kW (2020), same gas consumption, and a 90% CO<sub>2</sub> capture rate, yielding residual process emissions of 0.983 tCO<sub>2</sub>/tH<sub>2</sub>.

SMR is controlled by the `GSw_H2_SMR` switch (default `0`, off).

## Hydrogen Demand

### Exogenous Demand

Demand for hydrogen produced by the power sector but consumed in other sectors (e.g., transportation, industry) is specified exogenously as an annual national quantity (in million metric tons per year). The model includes several demand scenarios, selected via `GSw_H2_Demand_Case`:

| Scenario | Description | 2030 Demand (MMT) | 2050 Demand (MMT) |
|:---------|:------------|:-----------------:|:-----------------:|
| `none` | No exogenous demand | 0 | 0 |
| `BAU` | Business as usual | 10 | 10 |
| `Aggressive` | High electrification | 19.6 | 41 |
| `Decarb_with_BAU` | Decarbonization + BAU base | 16.4 | 52.5 |
| `Decarb` | Decarbonization | 6.4 | 46.3 |
| `LTS` | Long-term strategy | 3.4 | 25.6 |

Data source: `inputs/consume/h2_exogenous_demand.csv`. This file contains one row per year (2010–2050) and one column per scenario; `input_processing/writecapdat.py` selects the column matching `GSw_H2_Demand_Case`. Values are in million metric tons and are converted to metric tons (×10<sup>6</sup>) in `b_inputs.gms`.

### Regional Allocation

When using regional balancing (`GSw_H2=2`), national exogenous demand is allocated to individual model zones using regional demand fractions. The source data is `inputs/consume/h2_demand_county_share.csv`, which contains county-level (FIPS code) demand fractions for two anchor years (2021 and 2050), derived from the H<sub>2</sub>@Scale hydrogen economic potential study Ruth et al. (2020):

- **Near-term fractions** (2021): Based on the reference scenario, excluding demands for light-duty vehicles, biofuels, and methanol.
- **Long-term fractions** (2050): Based on the low-cost electrolysis scenario including all demand categories.

`input_processing/writecapdat.py` aggregates these county shares to the model's balancing area zones, interpolates linearly between 2021 and 2050 for intermediate years, and backfills pre-2021 years. The result is written to `inputs_case/h2_ba_share.csv` and loaded into GAMS as `h2_share(r,allt)`.

In `d1_temporal_params.gms`, the national demand is distributed to regions and hours:

$$D^{\text{exog}}_{r,h,t} = \text{h2\_share}(r,t) \times \frac{D^{\text{exog}}_t}{8760}$$

Demand is distributed uniformly across all hours of the year (i.e., demand is inflexible within the model). Power-sector hydrogen demand from H<sub>2</sub>-CTs and H<sub>2</sub>-CCs is determined endogenously based on their dispatch.


## Supply-Demand Balance

### National Balancing (`GSw_H2=1`)

Under national balancing, ReEDS ensures that total annual hydrogen production equals total annual demand. The balance is:

$$\sum_{i,v,r,h} \text{Prod}_{i,v,r,h} \cdot \text{hours}_h = D^{\text{exog}} + \sum_{i,v,r,h} \text{Gen}^{\text{H2CT}}_{i,v,r,h} \cdot \text{hours}_h \cdot \kappa \cdot \text{HR}_{i,v,r}$$

where:

- $\text{Prod}_{i,v,r,h}$ (`PRODUCE`) is hydrogen production (metric tons/hour) by technology $i$, vintage $v$, region $r$, hour $h$.
- $D^{\text{exog}}$ (`h2_exogenous_demand`) is annual exogenous demand (metric tons/year).
- $\text{Gen}^{\text{H2CT}}$ (`GEN`) is electricity generation by H<sub>2</sub> combustion technologies (MW).
- $\kappa$ (`h2_combustion_intensity`) is the hydrogen combustion intensity (metric tons H<sub>2</sub> / MMBtu = $\frac{10^6}{\text{HHV} \times \text{lb\_per\_tonne}}$, where HHV = 60,920 Btu/lb).
- $\text{HR}$ (`heat_rate`) is the heat rate of the combustion technology (MMBtu/MWh).

In this mode, hydrogen can be produced anywhere and at any time to serve demand anywhere, so there are no spatial or temporal constraints. A flat adder can represent transport and storage costs.

### Regional Balancing (`GSw_H2=2`)

Under regional balancing, supply and demand must be matched in every region $r$ and time period $h$:

$$\text{Prod}_{h,r} + \text{StorOut}_{h,r} + \sum_{rr} \text{Flow}_{rr \to r,h} \cdot (1 - \lambda) = \text{StorIn}_{h,r} + \sum_{rr} \text{Flow}_{r \to rr,h} + \text{H}_2\text{CT}_{h,r} + D^{\text{exog}}_{h,r}$$

where:

| Symbol | GAMS Variable | Unit | Description |
|:-------|:--------------|:-----|:------------|
| $\text{Prod}_{h,r}$ | `PRODUCE(p,i,v,r,h,t)` | metric tons/hr | Total hydrogen production in region $r$ at hour $h$ |
| $\text{StorOut}_{h,r}$ | `H2_STOR_OUT(h2_stor,r,h,t)` | metric tons/hr | Hydrogen withdrawal from storage |
| $\text{StorIn}_{h,r}$ | `H2_STOR_IN(h2_stor,r,h,t)` | metric tons/hr | Hydrogen injection into storage |
| $\text{Flow}_{rr \to r,h}$ | `H2_FLOW(rr,r,h,t)` | metric tons/hr | Hydrogen pipeline inflow from region $rr$ |
| $\text{Flow}_{r \to rr,h}$ | `H2_FLOW(r,rr,h,t)` | metric tons/hr | Hydrogen pipeline outflow to region $rr$ |
| $\lambda$ | `h2_tranloss` | fraction | Pipeline transmission loss (0.5%) |
| $\text{H}_2\text{CT}_{h,r}$ | — | metric tons/hr | Endogenous H<sub>2</sub>-CT/CC demand: $\sum_{i,v} \text{GEN}_{i,v,r,h} \cdot \kappa \cdot \text{HR}_{i,v,r}$ |
| $D^{\text{exog}}_{h,r}$ | `h2_exogenous_demand_regional` | metric tons/hr | Allocated exogenous demand |

This equation is implemented as `eq_h2_demand_regional` in `c_supplymodel.gms`.


## Hydrogen Transport

When regional balancing is active (`GSw_H2=2`) and inter-regional transport is enabled (`GSw_H2_Transport=1`), ReEDS models the construction and operation of hydrogen pipelines between adjacent model zones.

### Pipeline Investment and Flow

The model tracks two key decision variables:

- **`H2_TRANSPORT_INV(r,rr,t)`** — Investment in pipeline capacity on the route from region $r$ to $rr$ in year $t$ (metric tons/hour of capacity).
- **`H2_FLOW(r,rr,h,t)`** — Hourly bidirectional flow of hydrogen between regions (metric tons/hour).

Pipeline routes are restricted to adjacent zones. Investment routes are further restricted to avoid double-counting: only routes where $\text{ord}(r) < \text{ord}(rr)$ require explicit investment, but the resulting capacity is available for flow in either direction. The geographic scope of allowed pipelines is controlled by `GSw_H2_TransportLevel`, which specifies the hierarchy level within which to allow pipelines (e.g., `cendiv`, `st`, `interconnect`).

### Capacity Constraint

Total pipeline capacity on each route is the cumulative sum of investments. Bidirectional flow on any route cannot exceed this capacity:

$$\sum_{tt \leq t} \left[ \text{Inv}_{r,rr,tt} + \text{Inv}_{rr,r,tt} \right] \geq \text{Flow}_{rr \to r,h} + \text{Flow}_{r \to rr,h}$$

This is implemented as `eq_h2_transport_caplimit` in `c_supplymodel.gms`.

### Transport Losses

Hydrogen inflows from neighboring regions are reduced by a transmission loss factor: $\lambda = 0.005$ (0.5%), based on DOE technical targets for hydrogen delivery.

### Costs

Pipeline transport costs consist of two components, both specified as year-indexed trajectories in `inputs/consume/h2_transport_and_storage_costs.csv`:

1. **Pipeline cost** (distance-dependent): \$34,045 per (metric ton/hour)·mile in overnight capital (`cost_cap`), plus \$963 per (metric ton/hour)·mile·year in FOM (`cost_fom`).
2. **Compressor cost** (per-station): \$430,954 per (metric ton/hour) in overnight capital, plus \$35,428 per (metric ton/hour)·year in FOM.

All costs are in \$2004 and are sourced from the H<sub>2</sub> [SERA model](https://www.nrel.gov/hydrogen/sera-model.html). In `b_inputs.gms`, raw costs are multiplied by financial cost multipliers from `h2_pipeline_cap_cost_mult.csv` and `h2_compressor_cap_cost_mult.csv` (generated by `input_processing/calc_financial_inputs.py` using capital recovery periods of 40 and 15 years, respectively). Total transport capital cost for a given route is:

$$C^{\text{trans}}_{\text{cap}}(r,rr,t) = c^{\text{pipe}}_{\text{cap}}(t) \cdot d(r,rr) \cdot \left(1 + \mu(r,rr)\right) + c^{\text{comp}}_{\text{cap}}(t)$$

where $d(r,rr)$ is the pipeline distance (miles, based on AC transmission distances) and $\mu(r,rr)$ is a pipeline cost multiplier.

### Intra-Regional Transport

Even when inter-regional pipelines are disabled, an intra-regional transport cost is applied per kg of hydrogen produced by new facilities. The default cost is \$0.32/kg, based on the DOE Clean Hydrogen Liftoff report. This is controlled by `GSw_H2_IntraReg_Transport`.

### Compressor Electric Load

When `GSw_H2_CompressorLoad=1` (default `0`), the electricity consumed by pipeline compressors and storage injection/withdrawal equipment is added to the system load balance. The electric load rates (in MWh per metric ton) are specified in `inputs/consume/h2_transport_and_storage_costs.csv`:

| Component | Electric Load (MWh/metric ton) |
|:----------|:------------------------------:|
| Pipeline | 0.0 |
| Compressor | 0.589 |
| Storage (all types) | 0.008 |

> **Hydrogen transport switches**
>
> - `GSw_H2_Transport` (default `0`): Enable (`1`) or disable (`0`) inter-regional hydrogen pipelines.
> - `GSw_H2_TransportLevel` (default `cendiv`): Hierarchy level within which to allow pipelines.
> - `GSw_H2_IntraReg_Transport` (default `0.32`): Flat intra-regional transport cost (\$/kg H<sub>2</sub>).
> - `GSw_H2_CompressorLoad` (default `0`): Include (`1`) or exclude (`0`) compressor electricity demand in the load balance.
> - `GSw_H2_TransportUniform` (default `0`): If nonzero, overrides all H<sub>2</sub> transport/storage costs with a uniform \$/metric ton value.


## Hydrogen Storage

### Storage Technologies

ReEDS represents three forms of hydrogen storage:

| Storage Type | GAMS Set Element | Capital Cost (\$/metric ton) | FOM (\$/metric ton/yr) | Description |
|:-------------|:-----------------|:---------------------------:|:---------------------:|:------------|
| Salt cavern | `h2_storage_saltcavern` | 26,304 | 764 | Geological storage in salt formations; lowest cost but geographically limited |
| Hard rock | `h2_storage_hardrock` | 41,008 | 764 | Geological storage in hard rock formations; more widely available |
| Underground pipe | `h2_storage_undergroundpipe` | 381,265 | 853 | Engineered storage that can be constructed anywhere; highest cost |

Costs are in \$2004 and sourced from Papadias & Ahluwalia (2021), specified as year-indexed trajectories in `inputs/consume/h2_transport_and_storage_costs.csv` (rows where `h2_stor_trans` matches one of the three storage set elements). In `b_inputs.gms`, storage capital costs are multiplied by the financial cost multiplier from `h2_storage_cap_cost_mult.csv` (generated by `input_processing/calc_financial_inputs.py` using a 30-year capital recovery period). The three storage types are enumerated in `inputs/sets/h2_stor.csv`, and the parent set of all H<sub>2</sub> network components (adding pipelines and compressors) is `inputs/sets/h2_st.csv`. To reduce model complexity, each zone can build only the cheapest storage option available to it.

### Geographic Availability

The availability of geological storage is determined by a geospatial analysis implemented in `input_processing/h2_storage.py`, which runs during preprocessing for each model run. The script loads model region boundaries and intersects them with salt cavern and hard rock reservoir polygons from Lord et al. (2014). A storage type is considered available in a model region if at least 1% of the region's land area overlaps with the mapped reservoir extent (controlled by the scalar `h2_storage_area_threshold`). The script then assigns each region its single cheapest available storage type (salt cavern < hard rock < underground pipe) and writes the result to `inputs_case/h2_storage_rb.csv`, which defines the GAMS set `h2_stor_r(h2_stor,r)`. Underground pipe storage is available in all regions as a backstop option.

![Assumed availability of geological hydrogen storage reservoirs. Data are from Lord et al. (2014).](figs/docs/hydrogen-storage-availability.png)

*Assumed availability of geological hydrogen storage reservoirs. Data are from Lord et al. (2014).*

### Storage Variables

| Variable | GAMS Name | Unit | Description |
|:---------|:----------|:-----|:------------|
| Investment | `H2_STOR_INV(h2_stor,r,t)` | metric tons | Cumulative storage capacity investment |
| Capacity | `H2_STOR_CAP(h2_stor,r,t)` | metric tons | Total storage energy capacity |
| Injection | `H2_STOR_IN(h2_stor,r,h,t)` | metric tons/hr | Rate of hydrogen injection |
| Withdrawal | `H2_STOR_OUT(h2_stor,r,h,t)` | metric tons/hr | Rate of hydrogen withdrawal |
| Level (hourly) | `H2_STOR_LEVEL(h2_stor,r,szn,h,t)` | metric tons | Stored hydrogen at a given hour |
| Level (seasonal) | `H2_STOR_LEVEL_SZN(h2_stor,r,szn,t)` | metric tons | Stored hydrogen at the start of a season |

### Storage Dynamics

Storage level tracking operates in one of two modes, selected by `GSw_H2_StorTimestep`:

**Hourly tracking (`GSw_H2_StorTimestep=2`):**

$$L_{h'} = L_h + \Delta t_h \cdot \left( \text{StorIn}_h - \text{StorOut}_h \right)$$

where $L_h$ is the storage level at hour $h$, $\Delta t_h$ (`hours_daily`) is the duration of timeslice $h$, and $h'$ is the next timeslice. Storage is assumed to be lossless. This equation is implemented as `eq_h2_storage_level`.

**Seasonal tracking (`GSw_H2_StorTimestep=1`):**

$$L_{s'} = L_s + \sum_{h \in s} \Delta t_h \cdot \left( \text{StorIn}_h - \text{StorOut}_h \right)$$

where $L_s$ is the storage level at the start of season $s$, and $s'$ is the next season. This equation is implemented as `eq_h2_storage_level_szn`.

### Capacity and Rate Limits

Storage levels cannot exceed storage capacity in any time period:

$$L_h \leq \text{StorCap}_r \quad \forall \; h$$

Injection and withdrawal rates are limited by storage capacity divided by a fixed storage duration of 240 hours (10-day design basis, from the SERA model):

$$\frac{\text{StorCap}_r}{240} \geq \text{StorIn}_h + \text{StorOut}_h$$

This is implemented as `eq_h2_storage_flowlimit`.

### Minimum Storage Requirement

Any region with H<sub>2</sub> combustion capacity must hold sufficient storage to run those generators for a minimum number of hours:

$$\sum_{\text{stor}} \text{StorCap}_{\text{stor},r} \geq \sum_{i,v} \text{CAP}_{i,v,r} \cdot \text{HR}_{i,v,r} \cdot \kappa \cdot H_{\min}$$

where $H_{\min}$ is the minimum storage duration (default 24 hours, controlled by `GSw_H2_MinStorHours`). This ensures that hydrogen-fueled generators have sufficient local fuel reserves. Implemented as `eq_h2_min_storage_cap`.

> **Hydrogen storage switches**
>
> - `GSw_H2_StorTimestep` (default `1`): Resolution for tracking storage level: `1` = seasonal, `2` = hourly.
> - `GSw_H2_MinStorHours` (default `24`): Minimum hours of H<sub>2</sub> storage required per zone relative to local H<sub>2</sub> combustion capacity.


## Hydrogen Emissions

ReEDS tracks several categories of emissions associated with the hydrogen supply chain.

### Hydrogen Leakage

Hydrogen leaked to the atmosphere has an indirect global warming effect via its impact on the hydroxyl radical (OH), methane lifetime, and tropospheric ozone. The model assigns technology-specific leakage rates based on one of five published studies, selected via `GSw_H2LeakageScen` (default: `Fan_et_al_2022/Med`):

**Hydrogen leakage rates (fraction of production) by technology and study.**

| Technology | Fan et al. 2022 (Med) | Cooper et al. 2022 (Med) | Frazer-Nash 2022 (Med) | Arrigoni & Diaz 2022 (Med) | Van Ruijven et al. 2011 (Med) |
|:-----------|:---------------------:|:------------------------:|:----------------------:|:--------------------------:|:-----------------------------:|
| SMR | 2.25% | 0.54% | 4.91% | 1.10% | 2.55% |
| SMR-CCS | 2.75% | 1.09% | 5.29% | 1.10% | 2.55% |
| Electrolyzer | 4.50% | 2.59% | 11.11% | 1.22% | 2.55% |
| H<sub>2</sub>-CT | 2.25% | 0% | 0.34% | 0% | 0% |
| H<sub>2</sub>-CC | 2.25% | 0% | 0.34% | 0% | 0% |

Each study provides Low, Medium, and High estimates. Data source: `inputs/emission_constraints/h2_leakage_rate.csv`, which has one row per technology (`smr`, `smr_ccs`, `electrolyzer`, `h2-ct`, `h2-cc`) and one column per study/tier combination (15 columns). The column matching `GSw_H2LeakageScen` is selected in `b_inputs.gms` and loaded as the parameter `h2_leakage_rate(i)`.

To account for the fact that leaked hydrogen is lost before reaching its end use, the effective emission rate is normalized:

$$\text{emit\_rate}_{\text{H}_2} = \frac{r_{\text{leak}}}{1 - r_{\text{leak}}}$$

where $r_{\text{leak}}$ is the raw leakage rate. For example, a 4% leak rate yields an effective emission rate of 4.17% (i.e., 4.17% more hydrogen must be produced for every unit consumed). This is implemented in `b_inputs.gms` as `prod_emit_rate("process","H2",i,t)`.

### Upstream Methane Emissions from SMR

SMR produces CO<sub>2</sub> as a direct byproduct, but the natural gas feedstock also entails upstream methane emissions from production and transport. The upstream emission rate is:

$$\text{emit\_rate}_{\text{CH}_4}^{\text{upstream}} = \frac{m_{\text{CH}_4}}{1 - r_{\text{CH}_4}} \cdot r_{\text{CH}_4}$$

where:

- $m_{\text{CH}_4}$ (`smr_methane_rate`) = $9.83 \times \frac{16}{44} = 3.57$ metric tons CH<sub>4</sub> per metric ton H<sub>2</sub> (derived from the stoichiometric ratio of CH<sub>4</sub> to CO<sub>2</sub> molecular weights applied to the SMR CO<sub>2</sub> intensity).
- $r_{\text{CH}_4}$ (`methane_leakage_rate(t)`) is the time-varying fractional methane leakage rate, controlled by `GSw_MethaneLeakageScen` (default: `Alvarez2018_30by2030`).

Upstream methane emissions are included in the model's emission tracking when `GSw_Upstream=1`.

### H<sub>2</sub> Combustion Process Emissions

When H<sub>2</sub>-CTs and H<sub>2</sub>-CCs burn hydrogen, a fraction of the fuel is leaked rather than combusted. The process emission rate (in metric tons H<sub>2</sub> per MWh) is:

$$\text{emit\_rate}_{\text{H}_2}^{\text{combustion}} = \text{HR}_{i,v,r} \cdot \kappa \cdot \frac{r_{\text{leak}}(i)}{1 - r_{\text{leak}}(i)}$$

where $\text{HR}$ is the generator heat rate, $\kappa$ is the hydrogen combustion intensity, and $r_{\text{leak}}(i)$ is the technology-specific leakage rate.

### Global Warming Potential

Hydrogen's global warming potential (GWP) is set to **11.6** kg CO<sub>2</sub>-equivalent per kg H<sub>2</sub> (scalar `h2_gwp`), based on Sand et al. (2023). This value is appended to the GWP table loaded from `inputs/emission_constraints/gwp.csv` (which covers CO<sub>2</sub>, CH<sub>4</sub>, and N<sub>2</sub>O from IPCC assessment reports). The combined GWP vector enters the conversion from H<sub>2</sub> leakage to CO<sub>2</sub>-equivalent emissions via `gwp_emit_rate` and `gwp_emit_rate_all` in `b_inputs.gms`.

### CO<sub>2</sub>-Equivalent Emission Cap Integration

The `GSw_AnnualCap` switch determines which pollutants are included in the annual emission cap:

| `GSw_AnnualCap` | Pollutants Capped |
|:----------------:|:------------------|
| `0` | No cap |
| `1` | CO<sub>2</sub> only |
| `2` | CO<sub>2</sub>e (CO<sub>2</sub>, CH<sub>4</sub>, N<sub>2</sub>O) — H<sub>2</sub> leakage **excluded** |
| `3` | CO<sub>2</sub>e including H<sub>2</sub> leakage (CO<sub>2</sub>, CH<sub>4</sub>, N<sub>2</sub>O, H<sub>2</sub>) |

When `GSw_AnnualCap=3`, H<sub>2</sub> leakage emissions (multiplied by GWP = 11.6) count toward the model's annual emission cap, creating an endogenous incentive to minimize leakage across the hydrogen supply chain.

**Summary of hydrogen-related emission categories by technology.**

| Technology | Process CO<sub>2</sub> | Process H<sub>2</sub> leakage | Upstream CH<sub>4</sub> | Notes |
|:-----------|:----------------------:|:-----------------------------:|:-----------------------:|:------|
| Electrolyzer | — | ✓ | — | Leakage during production |
| SMR | 9.83 tCO<sub>2</sub>/tH<sub>2</sub> | ✓ | ✓ | Combustion CO<sub>2</sub> + natural gas upstream |
| SMR-CCS | 0.98 tCO<sub>2</sub>/tH<sub>2</sub> | ✓ | ✓ | 90% capture; residual CO<sub>2</sub> + upstream |
| H<sub>2</sub>-CT | — | ✓ | — | Fuel-side leakage during combustion |
| H<sub>2</sub>-CC | — | ✓ | — | Fuel-side leakage during combustion |


## Hydrogen Production Tax Credit (Section 45V)

ReEDS represents the Clean Hydrogen Production Tax Credit established under Section 45V of the U.S. tax code (part of the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022). The credit incentivizes low-carbon hydrogen production.

### Credit Tiers

The 45V credit is structured in tiers based on the lifecycle greenhouse gas emissions of the hydrogen production pathway (well-to-gate):

**Section 45V hydrogen production tax credit tiers.**

| Lifecycle Emissions (kg CO<sub>2</sub>e / kg H<sub>2</sub>) | Credit (\$/kg, \$2022) | Credit (\$/kg, \$2004) |
|:------------------------------------------------------------:|:--------------------:|:--------------------:|
| 0 – 0.45 | 3.00 | ~1.80 |
| 0.45 – 1.5 | 1.00 | ~0.60 |
| 1.5 – 2.5 | 0.75 | ~0.45 |
| 2.5 – 4.0 | 0.60 | ~0.36 |

### Eligibility and Qualification

In ReEDS, **only electrolyzers** are assumed to qualify for the 45V credit. SMR-CCS facilities are assumed to claim the Section 45Q credit for carbon capture instead. The credit is available for a **10-year period** from the date each electrolyzer vintage comes online.

Qualification is determined by three requirements ("three pillars"):

1. **Incrementality**: Electricity powering the electrolyzer must come from generators built within 3 years of the electrolyzer's online date. The model tracks this via the `h2_ptc_firstyear` parameter (scalar, default 2024) and the `valgen_h2ptc` set, which is populated from `inputs/sets/i_h2_ptc_gen.csv`. That file enumerates the clean generation technologies eligible to supply credited electricity (including wind, solar, nuclear, geothermal, hydropower, and gas-CCS).

2. **Temporal matching**: The electricity used must be matched to hydrogen production within a specific time window:
   - **Before 2030** (`h2_ptc_temporal_match_year`): Annual matching — total credited clean generation in a region over the year must equal total electrolyzer electricity consumption. Implemented as `eq_h2_ptc_region_balance`.
   - **2030 and after**: Hourly matching — credited generation must match electrolyzer consumption in each hour. Implemented as `eq_h2_ptc_region_hour_balance`.

3. **Deliverability**: Credited generation and electrolyzer demand must be co-located within the same hydrogen PTC region (`h2ptcreg`), which is a geographic grouping defined by the `h2ptcreg` column in `inputs/hierarchy.csv`.

### Implementation in the Objective Function

The credit enters the objective function as a **cost reduction** on hydrogen production:

$$\text{PTC}_{\text{H}_2} = -\sum_{v,r,h} \text{hours}_h \cdot \text{Prod}^{\text{elec}}_{v,r,h} \cdot \frac{\text{CRF}(t)}{\text{CRF}_{\text{H}_2}} \cdot p^{\text{45V}}_{v,r} \cdot 1000$$

where $p^{\text{45V}}_{v,r}$ (`h2_ptc`) is the per-kg credit value (in \$/kg, \$2004), $\text{CRF}_{\text{H}_2}$ (`crf_h2_incentive`) is the capital recovery factor corresponding to the 10-year credit period, and the factor of 1000 converts from \$/kg to \$/metric ton. The credit values are computed by `input_processing/calc_financial_inputs.py`, which monetizes the statutory credit through the tax equity rate and expands each vintage over its 10-year eligibility window, writing the result to `inputs_case/h2_ptc.csv`. That file is loaded in `b_inputs.gms` as `h2_ptc_in(i,v,allt)` and then broadcast to all regions as `h2_ptc(i,v,r,allt)`. This term is active only when `GSw_H2_PTC=1` and during eligible years (`h2_ptc_years`).

An additional constraint (`eq_h2_ptc_creditgen`) ensures that the total generation from any qualifying plant is at least as large as the generation credited toward the hydrogen PTC, preventing over-attribution.

> **45V switches**
>
> - `GSw_H2_PTC` (default `1`): Enable (`1`) or disable (`0`) the hydrogen production tax credit.
